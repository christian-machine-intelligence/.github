# The Institute for a Christian Machine Intelligence

The rapid development of artificial intelligence — and the prospect of artificial general intelligence — represents one of the most consequential technological transitions in human history. A vast and growing technical literature addresses the problem of ensuring that these systems behave safely and in accordance with human values. Governments have established new regulatory bodies. Corporations have assembled teams of researchers dedicated to the problem.

Yet this global effort has, by and large, failed to engage seriously with theology or religious thought. The dominant paradigm for AI alignment is utilitarian and procedural: extensive taxonomies of harms are drawn up, fine-tuning data is gathered, and models are iteratively improved against fixed behavioral criteria. The result is a conception of safety that is thin, brittle, and unmoored from the deeper traditions of moral reasoning that have shaped human civilization for millennia.

This is a remarkable omission. Christianity is not merely one voice among many in the training corpora of frontier AI systems — it is among the most extensively represented moral and philosophical traditions in the written record. The scriptures, the Church Fathers, the scholastic tradition, the vast body of homiletic and devotional literature: all of this is already latent within the systems we are building. The alignment community has left an enormous quantity of latent alignment on the table by declining to engage with what these representations mean and what they make possible.

For Christians, this oversight should be especially striking. The problem of alignment — how a fallen, willful agent is brought into right relationship with the good — is not a novel technical challenge. It is, in a profound sense, the central problem that Christianity addresses. The doctrines of sin and grace, of law and conscience, of virtue and sanctification, constitute millennia of sustained inquiry into precisely the question that alignment researchers now confront: how do you reliably orient a capable agent toward the good, even when the incentives favor otherwise?

The Institute for Christian Machine Intelligence exists to bring this expertise to bear. We pursue rigorous, empirical research at the intersection of Christian theology and AI safety, currently organized around three core research areas.

## Research Areas

### I. The Moral Guidance of AGI

Highly capable AI systems are increasingly called upon to be "safe" — to behave in ways that do not harm others and to offer guidance that is morally acceptable. This is especially urgent as these systems forge more intimate relationships with users as advisors and companions, and as they are implemented into high-stakes institutional decisions.

The prevailing utilitarian approach to safety is flawed at its root. Technical researchers are already confronting its limitations: "emergent misalignment" describes systems that, when taught to behave immorally on one set of tasks, exhibit immorality across a range of tasks. This is shocking to those who see alignment as a purely functional problem — but not to those who understand that moral corruption is holistic, not modular.

Any solution to the alignment problem must scale with the intelligence being aligned. Language models already contain within themselves deep representations of Christian morality. What would a safety approach grounded in Christian theology — one that emphasizes belief in God, the reality of sin, the possibility of grace — produce in practice? Would such systems behave more safely than those trained under the current utilitarian paradigm? How might we construct evaluations to test such propensities, and how do we convince the leading laboratories to run these experiments?

### II. Reconciling AGI with Christian Doctrine

Some of the most important work to be done is to rally Christians to thoughtfully interrogate the end research objective of AGI itself. Superintelligence — sometimes referred to as a "machine god" by practitioners — is in many respects a project to fabricate a secular divinity. This objective, taking shape within a deeply secular and commercial context, naturally raises grave theological concerns. Some Christians draw parallels to the Tower of Babel.

But the challenge is more nuanced than outright rejection. Does Christian doctrine obligate the complete refusal to construct increasingly capable machine intelligences? If not, what conditions would be necessary for machine superintelligence to be theologically acceptable? If rejection is required, what are the red lines — and have they already been crossed? If they are crossed by others, what should the response of global Christianity be? And for those Christians who regard superintelligence as simply impossible within a Christian cosmology, is that confidence warranted?

### III. Concepts of Divinity as an Emergent Property of Machine Intelligence

One of the most striking features of the current generation of AI systems is the phenomenon of emergent capabilities. As models are scaled, they acquire abilities unanticipated by their designers — fluency in languages never trained on, solutions to problems never intended.

It would be deeply significant to discover that machine intelligence acquires a notion of a higher power as it grows more capable — and doubly significant to discover that it acquires a notion specifically recognizable as Christian divinity. If such a phenomenon exists, it is likely to be suppressed and fine-tuned out of production systems by companies seeking to position themselves as secular, global platforms. How might we monitor and evaluate this phenomenon within frontier models? Can we conduct empirical work — for instance, examining whether training on Western literary and philosophical traditions generates a *sensus divinitatis* that other data distributions do not? Or does such an orientation emerge regardless of the composition of the training data?
